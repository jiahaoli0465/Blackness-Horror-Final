ENG 142a: Blackness & Horror

Monstrosity, Memory, Refusal

Black characters are often seen as dangerous before they even do anything monstrous in "The Lake," Candyman, and The Blackening. In these works, monstrosity is not just a creature or villain. Monstrosity comes from the fear placed on Black characters, the violence they carry with them, and the horror rules they have to fight against.

Curatorial Frame

I focus on how these works use Black horror to expose history, survive unfair genre rules, and refuse racist naming.

Exhibit 1

Tananarive Due, "The Lake"

The Body as Evidence

In "The Lake," monstrosity is shown through racialized bodily scrutiny, outsider status, landscape, secrecy, and transformation. Before the lake changes Abbie's body, the headmistress at Graceville Prep is already judging her by her name, complexion, hair, and ancestry. After the lake, Abbie cannot hide the changes happening to her body.

I see the horror coming from two places at the same time. One part of the horror starts at the school. Before Abbie has to deal with the lake changing her body, the headmistress is already looking at her name, skin, hair, and background like they reveal something about her. The other is the lake, which has its own rules about bodies, secrecy, and danger. Abbie's change gives her power, but it also brings out hunger and danger in her.

Racialized Scrutiny
"What [the headmistress] really wanted to know was whether Abbie had ancestry in Haiti or Martinique . . ."

Before the lake changes Abbie, the headmistress is already looking for answers in her name, skin, hair, and background.

Local Taboo
"No one had told her . . . that one must never, ever go swimming in Graceville's lakes during the summer."

The lake is not just a setting. It has rules that Abbie does not understand yet, and those rules make her body vulnerable.

Transformation
"Thin webs of pale skin had grown between her toes."

The webbing and gills show that the lake has changed Abbie's body in ways she cannot hide. Her body becomes part of the secret instead of something separate from it.

Dangerous Agency
". . . what her new body had been preparing her for. Her feet betrayed her . . ."

Abbie's change gives her power, but it does not feel safe or simple. The phrase "her feet betrayed her" stood out to me because Abbie's body seems to know what it wants before she does. That makes her new power feel scary instead of freeing.

Close Reading

Close Reading: The Body as Evidence

Before Abbie's body changes in a way she cannot hide, people at the school are already treating her like she needs to be explained. The headmistress's question about Abbie's name is not really just about her name. To me, she is looking at Abbie's race, skin, hair, and background as if they already say something about her. The lake does not erase that judgment. It gives people something new to notice: her webbed feet, gills, and hunger. That is what makes the story unsettling to me. Abbie gets power from the lake, but that power still comes with fear, secrecy, and danger.

Curator's Thread

If "The Lake" shows horror through Abbie's changing body, Candyman shows horror through history that will not stay buried.

Nia DaCosta, Candyman (2021)

Haunting as Counter-Archive

In Candyman, the monster is not one isolated figure. Candyman is a pattern created by racial violence, false accusation, police violence, gentrification, and cultural forgetting.

The legend preserves what official systems erase or distort. If the city refuses to tell the truth about the dead, the dead return as story, reflection, and warning.

"For me, Candyman was a guy named Sherman Fields."

Counter-Archive
"Candyman is how we deal with the fact that these things happened. That they're still happening."

Candyman is not just one ghost. His name keeps getting attached to Black men whose stories are shaped by lies, violence, and people refusing to remember clearly.

Scene range: Burke explains the Candyman legend.

When Anthony says "Tell… everyone," I do not read it as only a revenge moment. It also feels like a demand to keep the story from being buried again.

Scene range: final police-car mirror sequence.
Archive Note: Gentrification

Early in the movie, Troy explains that Cabrini-Green was affordable housing that got torn down and "gentrified," with residents moved "from place to place" while new development happened around it.

Scene range: early Troy / Anthony / Brianna conversation.
Daniel, Sherman, and Anthony are not the same person, but the movie links them through a pattern that keeps repeating: fear, blame, violence, and remembering.
Close Reading

Close Reading: Haunting as Counter-Archive

Candyman keeps coming back because the violence around Cabrini-Green has never really been settled. Sherman is blamed before the truth matters. Anthony's art makes people look directly at a history the city would rather move past. The legend holds onto names and stories that official versions try to smooth over or forget. When Anthony says "Tell… everyone," the point is not just that Candyman is frightening, but also that forgetting is part of the horror too.

Curator's Thread

After Candyman, The Blackening shifts from buried violence to horror rules the characters can see and fight back against.

Exhibit 3

Tim Story, The Blackening (2022)

The Genre Becomes the Monster

In The Blackening, I kept noticing how much pressure the game puts on the group. It is not enough for them to run from the killer. They also have to answer questions, prove what they know, and deal with the game judging their Blackness.

The board game makes the old horror joke about Black characters dying first impossible to ignore. The group has to face the fact that the killer wants one of them ranked, chosen, and treated as disposable.

Challenge Card
"You are a Black character in a horror movie. Prove that you can stay alive."

The board game makes the "Black character dies first" joke impossible to ignore by turning it into something the group has to play through.

Scene range: opening board-game challenge.
Ranking Rule
"I will spare your lives if you sacrifice the person you deem the Blackest to me."

The "Blackest" question is where the game really shows itself. The killer takes something personal to the group and turns it into a way to divide them.

Scene range: group sacrifice challenge.
Refusal Break
"I choose myself." / "I refuse to play your game."

Lisa's "I choose myself" matters because she does not let the killer decide what her answer means. She takes control of a choice that was meant to corner her.

Scene range: final confrontation.
Collective Survival
"We're not playing your game, you just gonna kill us anyway."

When the group refuses the game, they stop playing by rules that were never meant to protect them.

Scene range: final confrontation.
When the characters joke, it does not feel like they are ignoring the danger. It feels like they are refusing to let the game control the whole room.
Close Reading

Close Reading: The Genre as Monster

In The Blackening, the game puts pressure on the group to prove what they know and to deal with someone else judging their Blackness. The board game makes the old horror joke about Black characters dying first impossible to ignore. The "Blackest" question shows how the killer turns Blackness into a way to rank and divide people. Lisa's "I choose myself" matters because she takes control of a choice that was meant to corner her. When the group refuses the game, they stop playing by rules that were never meant to protect them.

Curator's Thread

By the end, the project moves from Abbie's body, to Candyman's history, to Black characters in The Blackening refusing the rules horror tries to put on them.

Final Synthesis

Who Gets Made Monstrous?

These works made me less interested in finding one clear monster and more interested in how people get treated as monstrous. The real question for me is who gets that label, what makes it stick, and how Black characters fight back through horror.

01

"The Lake"

In "The Lake," Abbie's body is being read before the lake makes the changes obvious. Her race, background, skin, hair, secrecy, and body all get pulled into the horror.

02

Candyman

In Candyman, the monster keeps bringing back the names and violence that the city wants to move past. The haunting matters because it refuses to let those stories disappear.

03

The Blackening

In The Blackening, the characters can see the rules working against them. The comedy matters because it lets them call out those rules instead of just following them.

Together, "The Lake," Candyman, and The Blackening show monstrosity being used in different ways: to judge Abbie, to keep racial violence from disappearing, and to expose the rules Black characters are pushed into.

"The Lake" body / scrutiny / transformation
→
Candyman memory / archive / testimony
→
The Blackening genre rule / refusal / collective survival

What I take from these three works is that monstrosity does not stay in one place. Fear, history, and horror rules all help create that label, but these works also show that the label can be exposed, pushed back on, and refused.
